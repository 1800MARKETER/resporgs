# State of the 8XX Inventory: A 2026 Look at Toll-Free Numbering Supply

_Of the eight Service Access Codes the FCC has opened for toll-free use, **the original 800 prefix has zero numbers available for new assignment**. 888 has only 61,935 available out of 7.7 million. The newest announced code, 822, hasn't been activated yet. We track every number in the Somos registry monthly — and the trend line is the same in every prefix._

## How the 8XX prefixes have rolled out

Toll-free service launched in the United States in 1967 with a single Service Access Code: **800**. As demand grew, the FCC opened additional codes:

| Prefix | Year opened | Reason for opening |
|---|---|---|
| **800** | 1967 | Original toll-free SAC |
| **888** | 1996 | 800 effectively exhausted of new vanity availability |
| **877** | 1998 | 888 exhausting at the same vanity-pressure pace |
| **866** | 2000 | Continued growth in 8XX-as-marketing-asset |
| **855** | 2010 | After several years of slowed openings; 866 inventory tightening |
| **844** | 2013 | First opening under the 4XX-codes-coming-faster era |
| **833** | 2017 | First SAC released via lottery / auction (FCC Number Reuse Plan) |
| **822** | 2024 (announced) | Most recent — TBD activation date |

Each new SAC adds ~7.4 million addressable subscriber numbers (8 × 10⁶ minus reserved blocks). On paper that's a lot of inventory. In practice, it isn't.

## Why the inventory exhausts faster than it grows

A toll-free number is a finite, named asset. There is exactly one **800-FLOWERS**, one **800-CONTACTS**, one **800-DENTIST**. Once a vanity word is taken in 800, the next-best substitute (888-FLOWERS, 877-DENTIST) is materially less valuable — the consumer has muscle-memory dialed 800 for thirty years.

This produces a structural imbalance in how the 8XX inventory exhausts:

- **Vanity inventory (the high-value slots) is exhausted in 800 and is approaching exhaustion in 888 and 877.** Our top-250 Master Million ranking for 800 finds ~98% of those positions held — the rare 2% that aren't are typically TRANSIT or DISCONN with imminent reassignment.
- **Numeric inventory (less-vanity-valuable numbers) is healthier in newer prefixes.** 833 and the upcoming 822 will absorb most net-new business assignments for the next 3-5 years.
- **Yet the total available pool keeps shrinking** — because numbers don't get returned at the same rate they get taken, and because ~33% of the most coveted slots are held by misdial-marketing operators whose business model creates no incentive to release them ([see "The Toll-Free Misdial-Marketing Industry Operates in a Regulatory Vacuum"](#)).

The headline:
- **800 has no available inventory.** Not low — zero. Every new 800 number must come from a disconnection.
- **888 is functionally exhausted.** 61,935 numbers available out of 7.7 million is 0.8%, the slowest-moving inventory of any active SAC.
- **The pool of "actually purchasable" toll-free numbers is 7.29 million** — roughly 16% of the total active inventory. This is the supply available to every business that wants to claim a new toll-free in America today.
- **844 is the most available prefix** at 32.6% pool — it's the workhorse for new business assignments right now.
- **833 grew the fastest** in the 4 months we have history for: +11.2% working count from January to April 2026, the highest of any prefix. It's where active business demand is going.

## Key signals to highlight in the published version

When the per-prefix data lands, the editorial frame should emphasize:

1. **The "real working" rate is misleading.** Of the 800 numbers tagged WORKING, our top-250 dial study (April 2026) shows only ~20% answer as identifiable real businesses. The rest are misdial-monetization, telco-warehoused silent inventory, or dead routing.
2. **AVAIL is not what's actually purchasable.** A number marked AVAIL in Somos data may still be in a 4-month "aging" period after disconnection (FCC rule), making the immediate-purchase pool smaller than the headline available count.
3. **Movement velocity matters more than static count.** A prefix with 200,000 SPARE that turns over 50,000/month is healthier than a prefix with 500,000 SPARE that turns over 5,000/month.
4. **Newer prefixes ≠ better quality.** 855, 844, 833 each launched with fanfare about relieving exhaustion. In each case, a substantial fraction of inventory was acquired by speculative resporgs and vanity brokers within the first 24 months. Same pattern, different decade.

## What this argues for

- **Public per-RespOrg holding metrics.** TollFreeNumbers.com / Resporgs.com already tracks each RespOrg's inventory size, growth, and disconnect-vs-acquire ratio (the "Opportunism Index"). Surfacing this prominently in industry policy debates would change the conversation about whether 822 should be released conventionally or via reform.
- **Time-bounded vanity holds.** A regulatory mechanism that requires demonstrated commercial use within N months of acquisition would force misdial operators to either run real businesses or relinquish inventory.
- **Visibility into the silent-AT&T inventory.** Of the 25 ATX01-held numbers in our top-250 sample, 11 (44%) gave silence-no-answer when called. That's a substantial pool of "warehoused" inventory not visible in any public report.
